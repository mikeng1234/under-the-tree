Under the Tree

Thoughts from the park with the large oval

I. Arrival

The spot

I found the tree — the big one by the oval — and unfolded my camping chair beneath it. The grass was soft enough to forgive me for sitting. The breeze came and went like someone checking in. The world, for once, felt unhurried. This was the spot. I knew it the way you know a good sentence — immediately, and without needing to explain why.

beginning
II. The oval

A small world

From here the oval is a planet. People orbit it — some fast, some slow, some with families trailing behind them like comets. Dogs sprint across the middle with no regard for gravity. Everyone moving in loops, and me at the centre of it all, perfectly still. A small world with its own rhythm, and I am the one thing in it that refuses to spin.

observation
III. Stillness

Anchored

There is something about sitting still while everything moves around you. The leaves rustle. The clouds drift. Thinking comes easier when you stop moving — or maybe it just gets louder. My bag sits heavy beside me like a second passenger, and together we hold the chair to the earth. It feels useless, just sitting here. But the tree does the same thing every day, and nobody calls it useless.

stillness
IV. The mind

Slowed down, sort of

The park bends time — stretches it thin like afternoon light. But the mind won't follow. Maybe it's the ADHD (self diagnosed, so maybe not). The body is still but the thoughts keep jogging their own oval, lap after lap. Risky choices. Old decisions. Roads I took because I didn't see the sign until I'd passed it. I sit here folded into a camping chair, trying to digest it all — and the park is patient enough to let me.

real talk
V. The others

What the joggers carry

What do they think about, the ones still circling? Are they outrunning something — a deadline, a debt, a version of themselves they don't want to meet at the front door? Or is it simpler. Maybe they think of dinner. Maybe they think of nothing at all. I envy that — the mercy of a quiet mind at full stride. They run in silence, and I sit in noise. We are all in the same park but living in different weathers.

wondering
VI. Absence

Fewer laps today

The park is quieter than past weeks. Fewer shoes on the path, fewer shadows crossing the oval. The Middle East crisis, the gas prices climbing — the world tightens its belt and the park feels it in absences. The ones who used to come every Saturday stay home now, doing the math on a tank of fuel. Even leisure has a cost. Even a walk in the park is a luxury somebody had to give up.

the world out there
VII. Golden hour — 5:17 pm

The kindest light

The sun hangs thirty degrees above the horizon — golden, unhurried in its leaving. Everything it touches turns warm: the grass, the path, the joggers' shoulders, my hands on the armrest of this cheap camping chair. It is 5:17 in the afternoon and the world is briefly, impossibly beautiful. Soon the light will go. But not yet. Not yet.

sunset
VIII. Disruption

Dust

Then — a car. Straight through the field inside the oval, no warning, no apology. Dust rises like a slow explosion, swallowing the golden light I was just holding in my eyes. The joggers stop mid-stride. The dogs turn their heads. The whole park holds its breath. And just like that, the spell breaks. All that's left is a brown cloud drifting across the oval where the calm used to be. The tree doesn't flinch. I suppose that's the lesson.

disruption
IX. Noise

Uninvited music

Birds are tweeting — sounds I don't recognise, small and strange and almost sad. I was trying to learn their language when the loudspeaker started. Somewhere across the park, a host leads a program, and their background music bleeds over everything — over the birdsong, over the breeze, over the quiet I came here to find. It is far, yes. But noise doesn't care about distance. It arrives uninvited and fills every corner like water finding the cracks in a room. One person's event becomes everyone's soundtrack. You don't get a choice. You just hear it. How easily the peace of many can be clouded by the act of one — and how little the one ever thinks to ask.
